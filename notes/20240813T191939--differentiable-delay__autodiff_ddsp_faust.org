* Fixed delay

Consider the case of delaying a function \(f(v)\), \(v = v(x)\), by
some fixed time \(\tau\):

#+NAME: eq:fixed-delay
\begin{align}
y = f(v) &= v(t - \tau).
\end{align}

The derivative of the above, via the [[denote:20240819T095042][Chain Rule]], is:

#+NAME: eq:diff-delay-1
\begin{equation}
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{dv}\frac{dv}{dx} \\
\end{equation}

But what is \(\frac{dy}{dv}\), i.e. the derivative of a delayed
signal?  First, the impulse response of the ideal delay system is a
time-shifted impulse:

#+NAME: eq:ideal-delay-ir
\begin{equation}
h(t) = \delta(t - \tau),
\end{equation}

and we can represent eqref:eq:fixed-delay as a convolution of \(v(t)\)
and that impulse response:

#+NAME: eq:conv
\begin{equation}
v(t - \tau) = v(t) \ast h(t).
\end{equation}

Next, recall the convolution property of the Fourier transform:

#+NAME: eq:fourier-conv
\begin{equation}
x(t) \ast h(t) \quad\xleftrightarrow{\mathcal{F}}\quad X(j\omega)H(j\omega),
\end{equation}

which we could also notate as:

#+NAME: eq:conv-mult
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F}[x(t) \ast h(t)] = \mathcal{F}[x(t)]\cdot\mathcal{F}[h(t)] = X(j\omega)H(j\omega),
\end{equation}

and the [[denote:20240819T103529][derivative property]]:

#+NAME: eq:fourier-diff
\begin{equation}
\frac{d}{dt}x(t) \quad\xleftrightarrow{\mathcal{F}}\quad j\omega{}X(j\omega),
\end{equation}

from which it can be deduced that:

#+NAME: eq:fourier-conv-diff
\begin{equation}
  \begin{rcases}
    \frac{d}{dt}(x(t) \ast h(t)) \\
    \frac{d}{dt}(x(t)) \ast h(t) \\
    x(t) \ast \frac{d}{dt}(h(t))
  \end{rcases} \quad\xleftrightarrow{\mathcal{F}}\quad j\omega{}X(j\omega)H(j\omega).
\end{equation}

To put it another way, using the notation from eqref:eq:conv-mult:

#+NAME: eq:diff-conv
\begin{align}
\mathcal{F}\left[\frac{d}{dt}(x(t) \ast h(t))\right]
  &= \mathcal{F}\left[\left(\frac{d}{dt}x(t)\right)\right]\cdot\mathcal{F}[h(t)] \\
  &= \mathcal{F}[x(t)]\cdot\mathcal{F}\left[\left(\frac{d}{dt}h(t)\right)\right] \\
  &= j\omega{}X(j\omega)H(j\omega)
\end{align}

Anyway, thus:

#+NAME: eq:diff-delay
\begin{align}
\frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{dv}{dx}\frac{d}{dv}(v(t) \ast h(t)) \\
  &= \frac{dv}{dx}\frac{d}{dv}(v(t)) \ast h(t) \\
  &= \frac{dv}{dx} \ast h(t) \\
  &= v'(t - \tau).
\end{align}

Or as a dual number, in discrete time:

#+NAME: eq:diff-delay-dual
\begin{equation}
\langle v,v' \rangle[n - k] = \langle v[n - k], v'[n - k] \rangle
\end{equation}

And in Faust:

#+begin_src faust :results none
diff(mem) = mem,par(i, N, mem);
#+end_src

* Variable Delay

This is where things get a little spicy. Consider a function of the
following form:

#+NAME: eq:variable-delay
\begin{equation}
y = f(\mathbf{v}) = v_{1}(t - v_{2}(t)), \quad v_{j}(\mathbf{x}) = v_{j}(x_{1}, x_{2}).
\end{equation}

It's derivative, via the chain rule, with respect to \(x_{i}\) is:

#+NAME: eq:variable-delay-deriv
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial{}y}{\partial{}x_{i}} = \frac{\partial{}y}{\partial{}v_{1}}\frac{\partial{}v_{1}}{\partial{}x_{i}} + \frac{\partial{}y}{\partial{}v_{2}}\frac{\partial{}v_{2}}{\partial{}x_{i}}.
\end{equation}

From eqref:eq:diff-delay we know that the first term reduces to
\(\frac{\partial{}v_{1}}{\partial{}x_{i}} \ast h(t) = v_{1}'(t - v_{2}(t))\). We have nothing in
particular to fear from \(\frac{\partial{}v_{2}}{\partial{}x_{i}} = v_{2}'\). Let's tackle the
remaining term, \(\frac{\partial{}y}{\partial{}v_{2}}\).

** Chain rule approach

By the chain rule, we have:

#+NAME: eq:chain-rule-dydv2
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial{}y}{\partial{}v_{2}} &= \frac{\partial{}y}{\partial{}u}\frac{\partial{}u}{\partial{}v_{2}}, \quad u = t - v_{2}(t) \nonumber \\
  &= \frac{\partial{}}{\partial{}u}v_{1}(u)\frac{\partial{}}{\partial{}v_{2}}(t - v_{2}(t)) \nonumber \\
  &= \frac{\partial{}}{\partial{}u}v_{1}(u)\cdot(-1) \nonumber \\
  &= -\frac{\partial{}}{\partial{}u}v_{1}(u).
\end{align}

Disappointingly, it feels like we're back to where we started (albeit
having introduced a negative sign), and that's essentially the nature
of the beast in this instance. Intuitively, the derivative of \(y\)
wrt. \(v_{2}\) involves the sensitivity of \(v_{1}\) to changes in
\(v_{2}\). There's no symbolic approach to this, and a numerical
approximation is almost certainly required.

Nevertheless, our complete expression for \(\frac{\partial{}y}{\partial{}x_{i}}\),
expanding eqref:eq:variable-delay-deriv, is:

#+NAME: eq:dydxi-via-chain-rule
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial{}y}{\partial{}x_{i}} &= 1\cdot{}v_{1}'(t - v_{2}(t)) - \frac{\partial{}}{\partial{}u}v_{1}(u)\cdot{}v_{2}'(t) \nonumber \\
  &= v_{1}'(t - v_{2}(t)) - v_{2}'(t)\frac{\partial{}}{\partial{}u}v_{1}(u),
\end{align}

where \(u = t - v_{2}(t)\).

** Symbolic integral approach

It's also possible to represent convolution (of continuous-time LTI
systems) with a symbolic integral:

#+NAME: eq:conv-integral
\begin{equation}
(f \ast g)(t) = \int^{\infty}_{-\infty}f(t - \tau)g(\tau)\,d\tau.
\end{equation}

Convolution is commutative, so the above could be written:

#+NAME: eq:conv-integral-2
\begin{equation}
(g \ast f)(t) = \int^{\infty}_{-\infty}f(\tau)g(t - \tau)\,d\tau.
\end{equation}

In the specific case of convolution with an impulse:

#+NAME: eq:conv-int-delta
\begin{equation}
(f \ast \delta)(t) = \int^{\infty}_{-\infty}f(\tau)\delta(t - \tau)\,d\tau = f(t),
\end{equation}

because, when \(\tau = t\), \(\delta(t - \tau) = \delta(0) = 1,\) and \(f(\tau) = f(t)\))
which demonstrates the /sifting/ property of convolution with the
delta function.

And with a time-shifted impulse [cite:@peacockFourier2013;
@wheelerDirac1997]:

#+NAME: eq:conv-with-shifted-delta
\begin{equation}
f(t) \ast \delta(t - k) = \int^{\infty}_{-\infty}f(t - \tau)\delta(\tau - k)\,d\tau = f(t - k),
\end{equation}

because, when \(\tau = k\), \(f(t - \tau) = f(t - k)\).

It's perfectly possible to convolve with the derivative of the delta
function:

#+NAME: eq:conv-with-deriv-delta
\begin{equation}
(f \ast \delta')(t) = \int^{\infty}_{-\infty}f(\tau)\delta'(t - \tau)\,d\tau,
\end{equation}

which can be solved via integration by parts:[fn:1]

#+NAME: eq:conv-with-deriv-delta-sol
\begin{align}
u &= f(\tau), \quad v = \delta(t - \tau) \nonumber \\
du &= f'(\tau)\,d\tau, \quad dv = \delta'(t - \tau)\,d\tau \nonumber \\
\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}f(\tau)\delta'(t - \tau)\,d\tau, &= f(\tau)\delta(t-\tau)\Big|^{\infty}_{-\infty} - \int^{\infty}_{-\infty}f'(\tau)\delta(t - \tau)\,d\tau \nonumber \\
  &= -\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}f'(\tau)\delta(t - \tau)\,d\tau \nonumber \\
  &= -(f' \ast \delta)(t).
\end{align}

The surface term vanishes because the delta function is only nonzero
for \(t = 0\). The above gives us the following nifty definition:

#+NAME: eq:conv-with-diff-delta
\begin{equation}
(f \ast \delta')(t) = -(f' \ast \delta)(t).
\end{equation}

For the derivative of a /time-shifted impulse/, we have this:

#+NAME: eq:conv-with-shifted-deriv-impulse
\begin{align}
f(t) \ast \delta'(t - k) &= \int^{\infty}_{-\infty}f(t - \tau)\delta'(\tau - k)\,d\tau \nonumber \\
  &\quad u = f(t - \tau),\quad v = \delta(\tau - k) \nonumber \\
  &\quad du = -f'(t - \tau)\,d\tau,\quad dv = \delta'(\tau - k)\,d\tau \nonumber \\
  &= - \int^{\infty}_{-\infty}-f'(t - \tau)\delta(\tau - k)\,d\tau \nonumber \\
  &= \int^{\infty}_{-\infty}f'(t - \tau)\delta(\tau - k)\,d\tau \nonumber \\
  &= f'(t) \ast \delta(t - k).
\end{align}

#+begin_quote
(One might expect the result to be negative, but apparently computing
\(du\) entails applying the chain rule to \(u\).)
#+end_quote

So, applying the result of eqref:eq:conv-with-shifted-deriv-impulse to
\(\frac{\partial{}y}{\partial{}v_{2}}\):

#+NAME: eq:troublesome-term
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial{}y}{\partial{}v_{2}} = \frac{\partial{}}{\partial{}v_{2}}v_{1}(t - v_{2}) = \frac{\partial{}}{\partial{}v_{2}}(v_{1}(t) \ast \delta(t - v_{2}(t))).
\end{equation}

Using the [[denote:20240819T103529][derivative]] and multiplication properties of the Fourier
transform, we know we only have to differentiate one of the functions
in the convolution:

#+NAME: eq:troublesome-2
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial{}y}{\partial{}v_{2}} &= v_{1}(t) \ast \frac{\partial{}}{\partial{}v_{2}}\delta(t - v_{2}(t)) \nonumber \\
  &= v_{1}'(t) \ast \delta(t - v_{2}(t)) \nonumber \\
  &= \frac{\partial{}}{\partial{}u}v_{1}'(u),\quad u = t - v_{2}(t)
\end{align}

and, putting it all together:

#+NAME: eq:dydxi-via-conv-integral
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial{}y}{\partial{}x_{i}} &= 1\cdot{}v_{1}'(t - v_{2}(t)) - \frac{\partial{}}{\partial{}u}v_{1}(u)\cdot{}v_{2}'(t) \nonumber \\
  &= v_{1}'(t - v_{2}(t)) + v_{2}'(t)\frac{\partial{}}{\partial{}u}v_{1}(u),
\end{align}

which isn't the same as eqref:eq:dydxi-via-chain-rule, so maybe that
computation for \(du\) above isn't correct, or I've committed some
other grievous mathematical sin.

** Visual intuition

Let \(v_{1}(x_{1}, x_{2}) = \sin(x_{1}(t))\), and \(x_{1}(t) = 2\pi{}t\).

#+begin_src python :session :results none :exports none
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np
plt.figure(figsize=(10,5))
N = 1000
t = np.linspace(0, 1, N, endpoint=False)
v1 = np.tile(np.sin(2 * np.pi * t * 1), 2)
plt.close()
#+end_src

  rate with fixed delay. E.g. constant delay of \(v_{2} = 0.1\):
