#+latex: \justifying

Spatial audio has an *accessibility* problem, but perhaps we can improve
the situation by *distributing* the signal processing. Wave field
synthesis, for example, parallelises quite nicely.

#+latex: \vspace{.5cm}\begin{center}\scalebox{2.1}{
\begin{tikzpicture}[font=\ultratiny]
  \pic {centralised};
  \node [single arrow, fill=red!50!black, inner ysep=3mm] at (9.75, 0);
  \pic at (11.5, 0) {distributed};
\end{tikzpicture}
#+latex: }\end{center}

**** Left
:PROPERTIES:
:BEAMER_col: 0.53
:BEAMER_opt: [T]

#+latex: \justifying

Distributing things in this manner requires that our group of signal
processors is *synchronised*, however, otherwise the resulting
soundfield will be destroyed.  A 1\nbsp{}ms asynchronicity between two
distributed audio devices would result in a propagation discrepancy of
over 30\nbsp{}cm.

*** Asynchronicity                                                  :B_block:
:PROPERTIES:
:BEAMER_env: block
:END:

#+latex: \justifying

We can configure a network of audio-capable devices (Teensy 4.1
microcontroller development boards) to run at a given target sampling
rate, but if we had a way of measuring /true/ time − and thus true
frequency − we would find that they're actually running *slightly
faster* or *slightly slower* than instructed.

#+latex: \vspace{.5cm}\begin{center}\scalebox{2.25}{
\begin{tikzpicture}[font=\supertiny]
  \pic at (0, 0) {teensythinks={nominalfs=48, actualfs=47.998}};
  \pic at (6, 0) {teensythinks={nominalfs=48, actualfs=48.001}};
  \pic at (12, 0) {teensythinks={nominalfs=48, actualfs=47.999}};
\end{tikzpicture}
#+latex: }\end{center}

Each device has its own *distinct internal time reference* − a crystal
oscillator (XO) − and each one of those time references behaves
slightly differently. XOs are rated to a given tolerance − typically
reported in /parts per million/ (ppm) − an amount by which they may be
expected to deviate from their nominal frequency.

*** Time Exchange

**** Left
:PROPERTIES:
:BEAMER_col: 0.58
:BEAMER_opt: [T]
:END:

#+latex: \justifying

IEEE 1588 *Precision Time Protocol* (PTP) is a nanosecond-order
time-exchange mechanism. Clock authority and subscriber(s) exchange
per-second timestamped packets until the subscriber has enough
information to determine its *drift* and *offset* relative to the
authority. These values are typically fed to a proportional integral
controller, and the result used as a /nanoseconds-per-second/
adjustment value to be applied to a dedicated PTP timer.

#+latex: \begin{center}\scalebox{2.3}{
\begin{tikzpicture}[font=\supertiny]
  \node [] at (0, 0) {
    \includegraphics[width=7cm]{./images/switch}
  };
  \node [draw=red!50!black, ultra thick, star, star points=10, rotate=15, fill=yellow, inner sep=.1mm] at (2, .75) {€ 200};
\end{tikzpicture}
#+latex: }\end{center}

Consumer-grade networking equipment does not tend to support PTP, but
relatively inexpensive exceptions do exist. What's more, our chosen
microcontroller platform supports it too.

**** Right
:PROPERTIES:
:BEAMER_col: 0.38
:BEAMER_opt: [T]
:END:

#+latex: \begin{center}\scalebox{2.3}{
\begin{tikzpicture}[font=\supertiny]
  \pic {ptp};
\end{tikzpicture}
#+latex: }\end{center}

\begin{gather*}
\text{Drift} = 1 - \frac{t'_{2}-t_{2}}{t'_{1}-t_{1}}\\[10pt]
\text{Delay} = \frac{(t_{4} - t_{1}) - (t_{3} - t_{2}) }{2}\\[10pt]
\text{Offset} = t_{2} - t_{1} - \text{Delay}
\end{gather*}

\small
The Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence measures the difference between two
probability distributions (i.e., the loss of information when one
distribution is used to approximate another). The KL divergence is thus
defined as
#
\begin{align} 
\label{eq:KL} 
\DKLPQ{P}{Q}{\|} = \sumin \Xoi{P} \log \frakPQ{P}{Q}
\end{align} 
#
with $P$ and $Q$ being two probability distribution functions and $n$
the number of sample points. Since $\DKLPQ{P}{Q}{\|}$ is not equal to
$\DKLPQ{Q}{P}{\|}$, a symmetric variation of the KL divergence can be
derived as follows:
#
\small
\begin{align} 
\label{eq:KL2} 
\DKLPQ{P}{Q}{,} = \sumin \Big(\Xoi{P} \log \frakPQ{P}{Q} + \Xoi{Q} \log \frakPQ{Q}{P} \Big).
\end{align}
